About


Fellow University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign student Ahmed Farooqi and designed a quadcopter during summer 2018. Since I am a mechanical engineering student and he is a computer engineering student, this was beneficial learning experience for each of us. We learned to work as a cross-disciplinary team of two. Overall, this was a fun project that helped introduce both of us to the design of products that utlize electronic components.

General Design

The first design was a simple symmetric frame that fit the dimension of the 3D printer being used.

I then created a center groove for us to place some of the electronics in. This allows for ample space for the propeller to rotate without hitting any of the electronics

Finally, I added holes in the center groove. This allows us to tie electronics down, while also making the quadcopter lighter.

I designed this frame to be easy to adjust and easy to print. This frame does not need to be put together piece by piece (as many frames do) and is ready to use right off the printer.

For 3D printing, we noticed PLA and ABS were both in a similar price range. After doing some research and looking at stress-strain diagrams of PLA and ABS, we found that PLA could handle more stress, but had an almost non existant yield point and did not strain-harden (aka it is brittle). The deformation that ABS undergoes before breaking is a good visual indicator that it is undergoing too much stress. We would not get this visual indicator with PLA.

Adjustments



After bringing our drone down to UIUC and setting it up for testing, we noticed a new issue where two of the motors were spinning at a very low RPM, and the quadcopter could no longer take off. After resoldering the motors to different sides of our ESC, we concluded our ESC was damaged during the process of moving.

Above is an image of us testing a motor. Before, this motor was barley able to spin, so we assumed it was faulty. After resoldering it to a different side of our ESC, it worked properly, telling us that the issue was with the ESC and not the motor. For future versions, we are considering using 4 seperate ESCs instead of a 4 in 1 ESC. This will make it easier to trouble shoot in the future. Also, it may be beneficial to edit the frame of the quadcopter to have a cover. I may re-design the body and create a snap-fit cover, so all the electrical components are hidden underneath a cover. This would be two solid "shells" that snap together, enclosing the electrical components.
